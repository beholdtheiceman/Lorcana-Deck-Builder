# Synergy Theory — Understanding Any Deck's Engine
_Not specific to one archetype. Apply this framework to evaluate any deck, identify what makes it work, and give precise gameplay advice based on the actual cards in front of you._

---

## What Is a Synergy?

A synergy is when two or more cards produce a result together that neither produces alone. In Lorcana, synergies usually work along one of five axes:

1. **Trigger chains** — Card A fires an "when played" or "when quests" effect that modifies the conditions for Card B
2. **Cost reduction** — Card A makes Card B cheaper to play (Shift, discount triggers, ramp)
3. **Stat modification** — Card A increases the strength or willpower of characters, changing what trades are profitable
4. **Resource generation** — Card A generates extra cards, ink, or lore that funds Card B
5. **Condition creation** — Card A creates a board state condition that Card B requires to function

Every competitive deck has a **core synergy loop** — a 2–4 card interaction that the deck is built to execute repeatedly. Understanding that loop is understanding the deck.

---

## The Five Synergy Types — In Depth

### 1. Trigger Chains

A trigger chain is a sequence of "when played," "when quests," or "when challenges" effects where each one sets up the next.

**How to identify it:**
Look for cards with "when played" effects that draw cards or modify characters. If the deck has multiple such cards, the player is trying to fire them in sequence — each trigger creates a larger board state or hand advantage than they'd have separately.

**Set 12 example (Amber/Ruby Toys):**
- Woody (5) quests → draw 1 → now have 5 cards → play a free 2-cost toy → that toy's "when played" fires → Jesse trigger shrinks a challenger → Babyhead gains power from the 2-cost play → now Babyhead is 5 strength and trades into their 4-cost
- Each step enabled the next. The chain created: 1 lore, 1 free character, 1 opponent character weakened, 1 favorable trade — from one quest action.

**How to play a trigger chain deck:**
- DRAW FIRST. Every trigger chain starts with maximum information. Don't commit ink before firing all draw triggers.
- Identify the "key trigger" — which card starts the chain? Protect it above everything.
- Hold "free" cards until the chain is ready to fire. Spending them early breaks the sequence.

**How to disrupt an opponent's trigger chain:**
- Remove the card that starts the chain. One well-timed removal on the turn before they would fire it costs them multiple triggers.
- If you can't remove the starter, prevent the prerequisite (e.g., keep their hand small so they can't "play" the free toy from the draw).

### 2. Cost Reduction Synergies

A cost reduction synergy allows the deck to deploy threats multiple turns earlier than their cost would suggest, or to play more threats per turn than the opponent can answer.

**How to identify it:**
Look for: Shift (the most common), "play for free" conditions, "cost X less" text, songs (sung by characters of equal/greater cost — effectively making the song free), ramp items, and cards that put ink into the inkwell.

**Categories of cost reduction:**

**Shift:** Playing a higher-cost version of a card on top of a lower-cost version, paying only the Shift cost. The key is that Shift costs are usually 2–3 ink less than the base cost AND the character enters ready (can quest/challenge immediately).
- Woody (5) costs 3 with Shift on top of Woody (1) = effectively an additional 3-cost 3-lore quester
- Prince Phillip shifted = paying 4 ink for a 7-cost character
- Elsa shifted = 4 ink for a 6-cost body

**Free toys (Ruby):** Characters with "you may play this for free when [condition]." These create multiple threats from a single ink investment.
- Hand in the Box, Wind-Up Frog = 0 ink characters that still quest and trigger abilities
- The deck plays these for free and holds them for the explosive turn — they're not "saved ink," they're "banked tempo"

**Songs sung by characters:** A character of cost X can exhaust to sing a song of cost X or less for free. This means:
- Bullseye (2-cost) + You've Got a Friend in Me (2-cost song) = Bullseye "pays" for the song, and you still have 2 ink available for other plays
- Any 3-cost character can sing Be Our Guest (2-cost) = the song costs 0 real ink

**Akood (Amber Steel ramp):** Reduces the cost of a high-cost character, enabling a 5+ threat on turns 2–3.

**How to play a cost reduction deck:**
- Identify the "reduction enabler" (the lower-cost piece) and the "threat" (the higher-cost piece). Protect the enabler.
- Plan 2–3 turns ahead. Cost reduction is most valuable when you hit your reduction condition exactly 1 turn before you want to play the threat.
- Don't "waste" cost reduction on the wrong target. Shifting Woody (5) is correct; shifting a 4-cost card you could have played normally is inefficient.

**How to disrupt cost reduction:**
- Remove the enabler before the threat can be deployed. Killing the Woody (1) before their Shift turn stops the Shift. Banishing Bullseye removes their free-song turn.
- Force them to "play" the threat at full cost — this costs them 2–3 ink more and potentially 1 full turn.

### 3. Stat Modification Synergies

Stat modification synergies change what trades are profitable by making your characters stronger or the opponent's characters weaker. This fundamentally changes which challenges are winning trades vs. losing trades.

**How to identify it:**
Look for: "+X strength" or "+X willpower" effects, "gets +X while [condition]," cards that shrink opponent's characters, Challenger +X keyword, and static auras that affect all characters of a type.

**Sub-types:**

**Permanent buffs (auras):** Characters with static "+X strength to all [type] characters." These change the combat math for every trade until they're removed.
- Woody (5): All your toys get +1 willpower — now your 2-cost toys have 3 willpower instead of 2. Suddenly Angel's recurring damage doesn't kill them.
- Lumiere tech consideration: +1 strength to toys — now they trade up into higher-cost characters

**Conditional buffs (combat triggers):** "Gets +X strength when challenging" (Challenger keyword) or "gets +X until end of turn."
- Jesse's ability: shrinks a chosen challenger — your 2-cost toy now trades into their 4-cost because their 4-cost is at reduced strength
- Babyhead's ability: when you pay 2 ink or less for a card, Babyhead gets +X strength — she's now a combat powerhouse for free

**Stacking modifiers:** Multiple sources of stat modification compound. Two Woody (5) on board = all toys have +2 willpower. Jesse + Babyhead in the same turn creates layered combat advantages.

**How to play a stat modification deck:**
- Know the exact combat math at all times. Identify which of your characters trades into which of theirs WITH and WITHOUT your buffs active.
- Fire your buffs BEFORE declaring challenges. Strength/willpower modifications are decided at the time of challenge resolution — buff first, then challenge.
- Protect your aura characters (Woody 5, Lumiere, Jesse) — they're changing every combat equation simultaneously.

**How to disrupt stat modification:**
- Remove aura characters first. An opponent's board without the aura character is trading at normal ratios, not buffed ratios.
- Sequence your challenges BEFORE they can fire conditional buffs. If Jesse fires before you challenge, you might walk into a bad trade. If you challenge before they play Jesse, you get the trade at normal odds.

### 4. Resource Generation Synergies

Resource generation synergies create more cards or more ink than a normal turn produces, extending the player's ability to keep playing threats long after the opponent has exhausted their hand.

**How to identify it:**
Look for: "draw X cards," "look at top X cards," "put a card from your deck into your hand," "add X ink to your inkwell," song tutoring effects, and items that generate value over multiple turns.

**Why resource generation is the most important long-game synergy:**
In a game where each player draws 1 card per turn, any deck that generates 2+ cards per turn eventually wins the card advantage race. A player with 6 cards has an answer to almost any situation. A player topdecking cannot respond to anything.

**Set 12 examples:**
- You've Got a Friend in Me: Look at top 4, add 2 toys = net +1 card beyond the draw (you effectively drew 2 this turn instead of 1)
- Jesse questing: draw 1 = this character generates a card every turn it's alive
- Babyhead: draw 1 when played = draws on entry
- Woody (5) questing: draw 1 = generates a card each quest cycle
- Multiple sources stacking: Quest Jesse → draw → quest Woody → draw → play free toy → Babyhead triggers = drawing 3+ cards in one turn

**How to play a resource generation deck:**
- ALWAYS activate draw effects BEFORE making ink/play decisions. More information = better decisions.
- Protect your draw engines above all else. A Jesse that quests 5 times generates 5 cards — that's worth more than a 4-cost character.
- Recognize when you have "card advantage debt" — if you spent your hand faster than you drew, you're now vulnerable. Draw before you spend.
- Identify which of your draw engines generates the MOST cards per turn and prioritize keeping that one alive.

**How to disrupt resource generation:**
- Remove draw engine characters on sight. A Jesse or Goliath that quests twice vs. zero times is a 2-card swing.
- Cards like Chromicon (hurts "when played" abilities) can disrupt entry-draw effects.
- Don't let their draw engines stay on the board at low lore — they're more valuable alive and drawing than they are gaining 1 lore per turn.

### 5. Condition Creation Synergies

Condition creation synergies require a specific board state to function — one card sets up the condition, and only then does the second card reach its full potential. This is the "combo" axis of Lorcana.

**How to identify it:**
Look for cards with "if you have X on the field," "for each [type] in your discard," "when you have fewer cards than opponent," and multi-card sequences that only work in a specific order.

**Set 12 examples:**
- Sid Phillips: "Banish one of your characters with 2 or fewer strength" → but only opponent's characters respond. The CONDITION is: clear your opponent's board of strong characters first, so their only remaining "choices" are their most valuable cards. The value of Sid is 100% determined by the board state at the time of casting.
- Bouncing Ducky: "Costs 1 less for each toy in your discard." The CONDITION is: having toys in your discard pile. The deck doesn't artificially put toys there — they go there naturally through combat. But sequencing matters: play Ducky AFTER combat trades, not before, to maximize cost reduction.
- Dale (YP Dale): All your follow-up characters get buffed. The CONDITION is: Dale is alive. Everything else in the deck becomes dramatically weaker without this condition being met.

**How to play a condition creation deck:**
- Identify which condition is hardest to create and protect it ruthlessly. If your "combo piece" dies, the rest of your hand is 40% weaker.
- Create the condition on the turn BEFORE you want to execute the combo. Not in the same turn — that burns your ink and timing.
- Have a backup plan for when the condition is disrupted. What does your deck do if Sid enters an empty board? (Answer: less efficiently — still fine, but not optimal.)

**How to disrupt condition decks:**
- Deny the condition, not the payoff. Removing Sid after he's already on the board is late. Denying the board state he needs is early. These are different decisions at different points in the game.
- Against Dale (YP Dale): Kill Dale, not Elsa. Without Dale, Elsa is just a medium character. With Dale, she's a 5-strength blowout machine.
- Against Ducky condition: Prevent their toys from reaching the discard (don't challenge small toys — let them quest; they'll never naturally die).

## The Core Loop Framework

Every competitive deck can be described as a core loop. When advising on gameplay, identify the loop first.

**How to find the core loop:**
1. What is the deck's primary win condition? (Quest to 20 via X, or burst to 20 via Y)
2. What card or combination makes that win condition consistent?
3. What enables that combination? (Cost reducer, search card, draw engine?)
4. What protects the enabler? (Removal, Ward, stat buffs, wide board pressure)

**Template:**
`[Win condition] ← enabled by [key combination] ← found/enabled by [engine piece] ← protected by [protection layer]`

**Amber/Ruby Toys example:**
- Win condition: Quest wide board to 20 (Pressure) OR Sid + Horseman combo clears and generates lore (Disruption)
- Key combination: Sid enters with minimal opponent choices + free toys flood the board
- Engine piece: Jesse/Babyhead/Woody draw chain (finds all pieces, maintains hand)
- Protection: Jesse shrinks challengers, Babyhead combat buff enables favorable trades, wide board splits opponent's removal

**Blurple example:**
- Win condition: Demona swings board after opponent over-commits challengers
- Key combination: Merryweather + Elsa + Demona sequence
- Engine piece: Hades for card draw, efficient challenges to build lore
- Protection: Ward (cannot be targeted), efficient challenges clear threats before Demona

**YP Dale example:**
- Win condition: Dale buffs + Elsa challenges create irreversible board advantage
- Key combination: Dale alive + Elsa shift
- Engine piece: Amethyst draw package, Genie resource generation
- Protection: Dale must survive — entire deck is built around protecting this condition

## How to Read an Unknown Deck Mid-Game

When facing a deck you haven't seen before, run this analysis after seeing 4–6 of their cards:

**Step 1: Identify their ink pair.** This eliminates entire categories. Amethyst has bounce and card draw. Ruby has direct damage removal. Emerald has cost reduction/disruption. Amber has healing and support. Steel has Bodyguard/Ward threats. Sapphire has ramp and tutoring.

**Step 2: Look for their density card.** What are they playing at 4 copies? That card is their core engine piece. If they've played Horseman twice in 3 turns, it's 4-of and central to their plan.

**Step 3: Identify the synergy type.** Are they making unusual trades that shouldn't work at face value? (Stat modification.) Are they playing cards 2 turns "too early"? (Cost reduction.) Are they rebuilding from an empty hand every turn? (Resource generation.) Are they sequencing carefully before a specific card? (Condition creation.)

**Step 4: Find the condition card.** What card, if removed, collapses the most of their plan? That's the target. Not the largest. Not the most expensive. The one their other cards need.

**Step 5: Determine if you're the beatdown or control.** Then apply your deck's playbook within that role, now with knowledge of what their deck needs to do.

## Synergy Evaluation for Deck Building

When evaluating whether a card belongs in a deck:

**Does it enable the core loop?** If yes, it's a priority include.
**Does it protect the core loop?** If yes, strong consideration.
**Does it generate resources the loop needs?** If yes, consider 2–4 copies.
**Does it work independently of the loop?** If it only works when the loop is active, it's a "win more" card — be cautious.
**Does it compete with the loop for ink?** If the card costs the same ink as your key combo piece on the same turn, one of them is getting cut when you need both.

**The "Good in 80% of situations" rule (from competitive play):**
Don't include a card that only answers one specific threat if it's dead text against the rest of the meta. A card needs to be useful in 80%+ of your matchups to justify its slot. Tech cards that win one matchup 5% more by losing another 10% are wrong cuts.

## Synergy and the Mulligan Decision

The opening hand determines which synergy path is available, not which one is "best." Evaluate your hand by asking:

**"Can I fire my core loop by turn 4–5?"**
- If yes: Keep. Even if suboptimal, you'll execute your win condition.
- If no: Evaluate what you're missing. Is it the enabler? The engine piece? The protection?

**"What am I missing and how likely am I to draw it?"**
Use probability:
- 4-of: ~40% chance in opening 7; ~65% by turn 3
- 3-of: ~31% in opening 7; ~53% by turn 3
- 2-of: ~22% in opening 7; ~39% by turn 3

If your missing piece is a 4-of and you have a playable hand, you'll likely find it. If it's a 2-of and you need it by turn 3, you probably won't.

**"Does this hand play the game or only work if I draw specific cards?"**
Hands that need 3 specific cards in sequence to do anything are wrong keeps. Hands that have multiple opening lines, even if none is optimal, are correct keeps.

## Putting It Together: Giving Real Gameplay Advice

When a player describes a game state or asks "was this correct?", the analysis should follow this order:

1. **Identify their deck's core loop** — what are they trying to execute?
2. **Identify which synergy type is dominant** — are they in a trigger chain moment? A stat modification trade? A condition creation setup turn?
3. **Determine their role** (from role-theory.md) — beatdown or control shapes everything
4. **Evaluate the board state** (from game-state-evaluation.md) — who's ahead, what type of ahead
5. **Apply synergy-aware sequencing** — given the synergy type active, what order of operations maximizes value?
6. **Name the correct play** — not "quest or challenge" as a binary, but "fire Jesse trigger FIRST, THEN use Babyhead pump, THEN challenge with the now-5-strength Babyhead into their 4-cost"

The goal is always: **Enable your core loop as early as possible, protect it as long as possible, execute it as often as possible.**
