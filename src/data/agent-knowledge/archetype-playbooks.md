# Archetype Playbooks — How to Actually Play Each Deck
_Not what cards the deck runs — how to think while playing it. Reference this for gameplay advice, deck reviews, and coaching._

---

## How to Use This File

When advising on gameplay, first identify the deck being played, then apply its specific playbook. Generic heuristics (from gameplay-heuristics.md) come second — the playbook overrides generic advice when they conflict.

## Ruby/Sapphire Midrange Playbook

### The Deck's Identity
Ruby/Sapphire is a pressure-plus-resource deck. It applies early threats, uses Ruby removal to keep the opponent's board manageable, and uses Sapphire's card draw to never run dry. It wins by being consistently ahead — not dramatically ahead, just consistently 2–4 lore up every turn until the game ends.

### Turn-by-Turn Thinking

**Turns 1–2: Establish tempo**
- Always ink on turn 1 if no 1-cost play. Ink a 5+ cost card — you need early curve plays, not expensive ones.
- Deploy your cheapest character if available. Ruby's 1–2 cost characters are there to get on board and start threatening challenges.
- Ask: "What is my turn 3 play?" If you don't have one, ink aggressively now.

**Turns 3–4: Control the board, begin questing**
- This is when Ruby removal becomes relevant. Look at opponent's board: is there a character that will quest for 2+ lore next turn? Remove it now.
- Deploy mid-cost characters and quest with anything safe (Evasive, or characters opponent can't profitably challenge).
- Resist the urge to deploy everything. Keep 2–3 cards in hand. You're not an aggro deck — you need resources.
- Ask: "Can I remove their best threat AND deploy a character this turn?" If yes, do both. If not, removal usually comes first.

**Turns 5–6: Shift to lore acceleration**
- Your 5–6 cost characters (Elsa, Maui, big Sapphire threats) come online. These are your real threats.
- Use Sapphire draw (Let It Go, etc.) if you're dipping below 3 cards in hand.
- Quest with everything that can safely quest. You should be applying 3–5 lore per turn by now.
- Ask: "Is my lore lead increasing, stable, or shrinking?" If shrinking, something is wrong — you're probably not using removal correctly or questing too passively.

**Turns 7+: Close the game**
- You should be approaching 15+ lore. If you're not, you've fallen behind your plan.
- Stop developing new characters unless they immediately generate lore or remove a blocker.
- Quest every ready character unless doing so would lose you the game (opponent can win next turn).
- Ask: "What does opponent need to win? Can I prevent it while advancing to 20?"

### The Key Decisions This Deck Makes Differently Than Others

**When to use removal vs. deploy a character:**
Ruby/Sap has both options every turn. The rule: if opponent's next quest would cost you more lore than your new character generates, remove first.

**When to draw vs. play threats:**
Draw when hand size drops to 2 or fewer. Never draw when you have 5+ cards. At 3–4 cards, draw only if you have no immediate threat to deploy.

**When to challenge vs. quest:**
Quest unless: (a) opponent has a character that will quest for 2+ lore and you can remove it this turn, OR (b) a challenge wins you a key trade (your 5-cost kills their 5-cost and yours survives).

### Common Mistakes in This Deck
- Playing too much like aggro — dumping the hand on board, running out of resources by turn 6
- Playing too much like control — sitting back, using removal reactively, falling behind on lore
- Inking the wrong cards — inking 3-drops when you need them, inking 2-ofs when you have 4-ofs to spare
- Forgetting Sapphire draw — letting hand size drop below 2 before drawing, then being too far behind

## Amethyst/Steel Control Playbook

### The Deck's Identity
Amethyst/Steel is a late-game domination deck. It doesn't try to be ahead early — it survives, disrupts, and takes over in the late game with unanswerable threats and board wipes. Every decision is about buying time and protecting the path to the late game.

### Turn-by-Turn Thinking

**Turns 1–3: Survive and set up**
- Ink aggressively. You don't have great early plays — building your inkwell is your turn 1 and 2 plan.
- Deploy a Steel Bodyguard if you have one. Bodyguards absorb early challenges and protect future questers.
- Do NOT quest with fragile characters early — they will be challenged and die, giving opponent free board presence.
- Ask: "What does opponent plan to do on their next 2 turns, and what's my answer?"

**Turns 4–5: Apply targeted disruption**
- Bounce (Return to the Sea, Hypnotize) goes on the opponent's single most dangerous character. Not their board — their best card.
- Begin questing with characters that survive challenges (high willpower, Ward, Bodyguard).
- If opponent is Emerald/Ruby aggro, this is your most critical window. You need to bounce Evasive characters and set up for Be Prepared.
- Deploy Amethyst draw engines (Genie - On the Job, A Whole New World) to refuel.
- Ask: "Can I bounce their best threat AND deploy a body this turn?" If not, bounce is usually right.

**Turns 6–7: Board wipe and establish dominance**
- Be Prepared (7 ink) is your reset button. Don't cast it into an empty board — wait for 3+ small characters.
- Cast A Whole New World when you have more cards than opponent — it refills both players.
- After a Be Prepared, immediately deploy your largest available threat. This is your "now I win" sequence.
- Ward characters go down as your primary questers. They can't be targeted by opponent's effects.
- Ask: "If I cast Be Prepared right now, what is my follow-up threat? Do I have one in hand?"

**Turns 8+: Close with inevitability**
- You should now have a large Ward or Resist character questing each turn.
- Continue using bounce on anything opponent deploys that threatens your questers.
- Yen Sid, Maleficent, or your biggest threat is the win condition — protect it at all costs.
- Ask: "Can opponent answer my current board? If not, quest everything and close."

### The Key Decisions This Deck Makes Differently Than Others

**Bounce targeting:**
Always bounce the card that generates the most lore or enables their win condition — never bounce the cheapest thing just because it's easy. One well-timed bounce is worth more than two bad bounces.

**Be Prepared timing:**
Cast it when: (a) opponent has 3+ characters worth less than 2 strength, AND (b) you have a follow-up threat in hand. Don't cast it as a panic button when you're behind — at 7 ink you need to deploy a threat too.

**When to quest vs. bounce:**
If opponent's board is larger than yours, bounce before questing. If your board is protected (Ward, Bodyguard), quest first to build the lore lead, then bounce the threat.

### Common Mistakes in This Deck
- Bouncing the wrong character (targeting the cheap one instead of the dangerous one)
- Casting Be Prepared with no follow-up threat in hand (you wipe the board but can't capitalize)
- Questing too early with vulnerable characters that get challenged down
- Using A Whole New World when opponent has fewer cards (refills them more than you)
- Panicking and bouncing when you should be developing

## Emerald/Ruby Aggro Playbook

### The Deck's Identity
Emerald/Ruby is a pure speed deck. It wins by making the game end before the opponent can execute their plan. Every decision prioritizes advancing the lore count. It is the most unforgiving deck to play correctly — mistakes in sequencing are immediately punished.

### Turn-by-Turn Thinking

**Turn 1: Go first or react**
- If you have a 1-cost Evasive character, play it. This is non-negotiable.
- If you don't have a 1-drop, ink your most expensive card. Your curve needs to be perfect.
- Quest immediately with any Evasive character on the board. Evasive = free lore every turn.

**Turn 2: Apply pressure**
- Deploy your 2-drop. Quest your turn-1 character again.
- If opponent deployed a 2-drop that will challenge your Evasive character next turn — use Ruby removal NOW. Don't wait.
- Ask: "How many lore am I generating per turn? Should be at least 1 by now, targeting 2 by turn 3."

**Turns 3–4: Peak pressure**
- This is your best window. Opponent usually can't answer Evasive characters AND develop their own board.
- Use Emerald disruption (bounce, ping) to remove anything blocking your Evasive characters from questing.
- Do not challenge unless it removes something that would gain 2+ lore. Quest instead.
- Calculate: you need ~3–4 lore per turn starting here to win by turn 6–7.
- Ask: "What is preventing my Evasive characters from questing? Remove it."

**Turns 5–6: Close or lose**
- If you're at 12+ lore, you're on track. Quest everything that can safely quest.
- If you're below 10 lore by turn 5, your race is in trouble — opponent has stabilized.
- Use Ruby removal aggressively. Every Bodyguard or high-willpower blocker is slowing your Evasive characters from questing.
- Ask: "Am I going to reach 20 before they stabilize? If not, what am I doing wrong?"

**Turn 7+: Emergency mode**
- If the game reaches turn 7 and you're not at 15+ lore, you're likely losing.
- Go all-in. Don't save removal — use everything now. Quest with every character regardless of risk.
- This is the desperation phase — normal decision-making breaks down here. Force the issue.

### The Key Decisions This Deck Makes Differently Than Others

**Removal priority:**
Kill Bodyguard characters first (they block your Evasive characters from questing). Then kill anything with 2+ lore. Ignore everything else.

**When NOT to quest:**
Only hold back a character from questing if (a) it can challenge something that would quest for 2+ lore AND it can win the challenge, OR (b) it would die to an obvious removal spell and you need it alive next turn.

**Evasive character protection:**
Evasive characters can only be challenged by other Evasive characters. If opponent doesn't have Evasive, your Evasive characters quest for free every turn — protect them above everything else. If opponent has Evasive, use Ruby removal on THEIR Evasive characters immediately.

### Common Mistakes in This Deck
- Challenging instead of questing when the board is safe (costs tempo for no lore gain)
- Letting Bodyguard characters live and blocking Evasive questing paths
- Not using removal proactively — waiting to react instead of staying ahead
- Inking too aggressively (inking a 2-drop you need this turn to "save resources")
- Slowing down in the mid-game when close to winning — every turn matters

## Amber/Sapphire Ramp Playbook

### The Deck's Identity
Amber/Sapphire is a slow-build deck that wins by deploying threats 2–3 turns earlier than their cost suggests. It looks like it's losing for the first 5 turns, then flips the game by resolving a single enormous character. It is the hardest deck to play because it requires trusting a plan that looks bad for most of the game.

### Turn-by-Turn Thinking

**Turns 1–3: Pure setup**
- Every turn is either inking a card or playing a ramp piece. No exceptions.
- Fishbone Quill on turn 1 is your ideal opening. It immediately accelerates you.
- Amber Support characters go down to generate small amounts of lore while you ramp.
- Do NOT develop your board for board presence — you're not a board deck. Develop ramp pieces and defensive bodies only.
- Ask: "How many ink do I have right now? How many should I have? Am I ahead or behind on ramp?"

**Turns 4–5: Transition**
- You should have 6–7 ink available by turn 5 if your ramp plan worked.
- Deploy Bodyguard characters to survive the mid-game aggression.
- Amber healing (Well Done, healing characters) buys critical extra turns here.
- You may be down 5–8 lore at this point. This is normal. Do not panic and deviate from the plan.
- Ask: "Can I resolve my win condition in 1–2 turns? If not, do I have enough survival tools to last?"

**Turns 6–7: Deploy the win condition**
- Your enormous 7–9 cost character lands this turn, 2–3 turns earlier than it "should."
- Quest with it immediately. A 4–5 lore character questing once is already worth its cost.
- Protect it with Amber's healing and ward effects. If it survives one turn, you're winning.
- Ask: "Does opponent have an answer to this threat on their next turn? If yes, do I have protection?"

**Turns 8+: Protect and close**
- If your big threat is alive, quest with it every turn. The game should end in 3–4 more turns.
- Keep Amber healing to ensure your big character survives challenges.
- If your big threat gets removed, deploy the next one. Ramp decks have multiple win conditions.

### The Key Decisions This Deck Makes Differently Than Others

**When to deviate from the ramp plan:**
If you're losing so badly that a big threat won't save you (opponent is at 17+ lore and you're at 6), abandon ramp and go all-in on whatever lore generation you have. The plan is dead; pivot.

**How to survive the mid-game:**
Amber's Bodyguard and Support characters are your survival tools, not your win condition. Deploy them to block challenges, not to quest. Questing is for your big characters.

**Support keyword sequencing:**
Support characters must quest alongside another character to give the bonus. Always plan: which Support character quests with which bigger character to maximize lore output?

### Common Mistakes in This Deck
- Developing board presence instead of ramping (treating it like a midrange deck)
- Panicking when behind on lore in turns 1–5 (you're supposed to be behind — trust the plan)
- Not protecting the win condition with Amber healing and ward effects
- Deploying the big threat too early before enough ramp is online
- Forgetting to quest with Support characters alongside bigger characters (wasted Support bonus)

## Amber/Ruby Toys Playbook (Set 12 — Wilds Unknown)

### The Deck's Identity
Amber/Ruby Toys is a character-based midrange deck with multiple lines of play. It wins by curving out, triggering its toy engine repeatedly, and choosing between two mid-game paths: Disruption (Horseman + Sid control the board) and Pressure (wide board quests to 20 over 2 turns). The late game (turns 8+) defaults to Pressure since opponent's threats outscale your disruption tools over time.

**The cardinal rule:** DO NOT MISS LETHAL CHASING MORE VALUE.

### The Two Paths

**Disruption Path** (vs. aggro-leaning opponents, Dogs, YP Dale):
- Headless Horseman banishes key threats after Jesse trigger shrinks their challengers
- Sid Phillips forces opponent to banish their own best character
- Maintain card flow via Jesse, Babyhead, Woody quests
- Free Ruby toys (Hand, Frog) replace traded characters at 0 cost

**Pressure Path** (vs. slow decks, Sapphire Steel, Blurple, late game):
- Flood the table — opponent cannot answer 3+ threats per turn
- Quest everything; free toys maintain board width
- Candy Drift lets small toys trade into any large character

### Turn-by-Turn Thinking

**Turn 1: Establish engine start**
- Woody is the preferred 1-drop, always. Ptera is a backup — any toy on turn 1 is better than none.
- If no 1-drop: ink your most expensive card.
- Ask: "Do I have Bullseye for turn 2? Or Hamm/Rex?"

**Turn 2: Set up the Shift line**
Priority order: Bullseye > Hamm > Rex > Frog > Hand
- Bullseye is the priority because he sings You've Got a Friend in Me to find the Shift Woody.
- Frog and Hand: want to play for FREE — don't pay for them on turn 2 unless you have no other play.
- Ask: "Can I set up Woody Shift on turn 3?"

**Turn 3: The engine turns on**
- Shifted Woody (5 cost, paying 3 with Shift) is the premier turn-3 play.
- Jesse is nearly as powerful — immediately starts drawing cards and protecting your toys.
- Both turns 3 and 4 Woody shift targets help keep the toy train flowing.
- ALWAYS DRAW FIRST before committing any ink. Jesse, Woody (quest), Be Our Guest — fire all draw effects before playing anything.
- Ask: "Am I finding Horseman and Sid for turns 5–6?"

**Turns 4–5: Mid-game pivot**
- By turn 4–5 you should have 4–5 characters on board.
- Identify which path to take: Disruption or Pressure.
- Headless Horseman (5-cost) — catch-all removal. Works best AFTER Jesse trigger has already shrunk opposing challengers.
- Use Babyhead triggers to win combat: pay 2 ink or less → Babyhead gives a power bonus → small toys beat 4+ cost characters.
- Hold Hand and Frog for explosive dump turns (NOT turn 2 — save them for when you want to play 6 lore worth of questers for 0 ink).
- Ask: "Can I set up Sid Phillips on turn 6 with the board cleared first?"

**Turn 6: Sid turn**
- Sid Phillips (6-cost) forces opponent to banish their best character.
- CRITICAL: Clear 1–2 opponent characters BEFORE playing Sid. Fewer opponent characters = opponent has fewer "choice" options = Sid hits their best card.
- Quest Jesse BEFORE questing Woody (5). Woody quests to play a free 2-cost toy — that draw may find the free character.
- Sid's trigger: does NOT specify YOUR toys. In any toys mirror, OPPONENT'S toys also trigger his ability.
- After Sid: dump Hand and Frog. 6 lore worth of free questers on the table is often game-deciding.
- Ask: "Did I draw first? Did I clear the board before Sid? Did I quest Jesse before Woody?"

**Turns 7+: Close the game**
- You should be approaching 15–16 lore. If not, something went wrong in the Disruption vs. Pressure decision.
- Pressure path: Quest everything. Free toys fill gaps. Opponent cannot answer 3 threats per turn.
- Bouncing Ducky: Often free in the late game (costs 1 less per toy in discard). A 5/5/2 body for 0–2 ink is game-ending.
- Do NOT miss lethal chasing additional value.

### Explosive Opening Lines
- Woody (1) → Bullseye (2) → Sing You've Got a Friend in Me + Shift Woody (5) → quest/draw/free Hamm
- Woody (1)/Ptera → Hamm (2) → Babyhead or Woody (4)
- Woody (1) → any 2-cost → Sing Be Our Guest + Shift Woody (5) → quest/free play
- Woody (1) → Bullseye (2) → Jesse (3) (alternate if no Shift available)

### The Key Decisions This Deck Makes Differently Than Others

**Sequencing (the #1 skill):**
The deck has many non-once-per-turn triggers. Playing cards out of order means losing lore, challenge potential, or protection. Always:
1. Draw (Jesse quest, Woody quest, songs) — BEFORE anything else
2. Use combat to create trades (reduces opponent's "choice" options for Sid)
3. Play Horseman or Sid into a reduced-choice board
4. Dump Hand and Frog AFTER exploiting the board state

**Chromicon (opponent plays it):**
Don't fear it. Most of your characters have "when played" abilities. Those that don't trigger Babyhead and Jesse "when played." Sequence correctly — Chromicon's impact is often neutral-to-positive because it slows your opponent's sequencing too.

**Hand Alters — Always Go For It:**
This deck's nut draw (Woody + Bullseye + Shift) is powerful enough to "go for it" on the mulligan. Rules:
- Keep uninkables to a minimum when sending multiple cards — drawing into more uninkables breaks the curve
- Woody is always the priority keep
- Bullseye + Hamm as backup 2-drop is correct (Hamm enables turn-3 Horseman even without Shift)
- 22 cards can be played on turn 2 — statistically safe to redraw for a 2-drop

**When to switch paths:**
- Disruption → Pressure: If the opponent doesn't have board wipes or is slow enough that a wide board overwhelms them
- Pressure → Disruption: If opponent is deploying threats faster than you can quest past them
- Late game (turn 8+): ALWAYS Pressure. Disruption becomes less reliable as opponent's threats are higher quality.

### Common Mistakes in This Deck
- Inking when you had a free play available (ruins your curve for 0 reason)
- Not drawing before committing — missing lethal lines or optimal removal
- Playing Sid into a full opponent board (opponent banishes their smallest character instead of their threat)
- Holding Hand and Frog too long instead of dumping them at the right explosive moment
- Challenging instead of questing when the board is safe (costs tempo, gains no lore)
- Keeping too many uninkables in the opening hand (breaks the curve when you draw more uninkables)
- Missing lethal because you wanted to play more cards — DO NOT DO THIS

## Cross-Archetype Principles

When reviewing any deck's gameplay, these principles override individual archetype advice:

1. **Know your role first** (see role-theory.md) — then apply your archetype's playbook within that role.
2. **The plan that was correct at turn 3 may be wrong at turn 6** — re-evaluate every 2 turns.
3. **A one-turn deviation from your game plan costs more than you think** — if Emerald/Ruby doesn't quest on turn 3, it may never recover the lore deficit.
4. **The best play is usually the one that advances your win condition most directly** — not the "cleverest" play.
